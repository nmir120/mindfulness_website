What Is Meditation and How Does It Make You More Mindful?

Meditation is a practice in which an individual uses a set of techniques to develop control over their attention and awareness, by focusing on elements of the present moment. Meditating, in a sense, is like taking a moment to step out of the everlasting mental race that goes on in your head. It's about taking time to observe and learn from thought patterns rather than be reactive to them. Through this process, you directly and indirectly gain new perspectives about your thinking habits, which will make you more mindful of them.

With this boosted mindfulness, you are better equipped to respond more efficiently to various situations you encounter in your daily life. For instance, perhaps after meditating for some time and developing mindfulness, you might realize that you don't even enjoy your morning coffee and that it makes you anxious and jittery, so you stop drinking coffee and save a few dollars every day (based on a true story!).

Guided Meditation

Guided meditations are a great way to dip your toes in the water and get a feel for what meditation is like. Typically, these meditations consist of a voice recording in which the listener is guided into present-moment awareness, then instructed to bring their attention to a particular "object", which is commonly their own breath. Intermittently, the recording prompts the listener to redirect their attention back to the object of focus if their mind has wandered. These recordings are often accompanied with a calming soundtrack. There are countless guided meditations available online, many of them completely free. Guided meditations also come in various themes, which is great for targeting specific problems that you may be experiencing (e.g., grief, relationship stress, injuries). Two fantastic apps that have a wide selection of guided meditation programs are Headspace and Calm.

Breathing-Focused Meditation (Self-Guided)

This style of meditation is basic, yet deeply rewarding. As you might guess by the name of it, in this practice, the meditator brings their attention to their breathing. More specifically, focus is placed on the sensations produced by breathing, for example, at the opening of the dominant nostril. So why should you use this technique? Firstly, as this practice is self-guided, you will not be interrupted by a guided voice recording, allowing you to sink deeper into a concentrated state. Secondly, the breath is a staple object to train your attention with; it is always present and it provides enough stimulation for you to keep your mind busy. Below, I have outlined eight simple steps you can take to begin a breathing-focused meditation session.

  1. Decide how long you would like to meditate and set your timer or alarm. For beginners, I would recommend a daily 10-15 minute session. You can increment the length of your sessions over time to eventually sit for 45 minutes to one hour long sessions, which is recommended for deep concentration and mindfulness development.
  2. Find a comfortable sitting position where you can keep your spine tall (try sitting at the end of your chair, where your hips are at a higher level than your knees).
  3. Gently close your eyes.
  4. Become aware of your physical surroundings (sounds, smells, temperature, etc.)
  5. Become aware of your bodily sensations (heaviness, warmth, coolness, tension, softness, etc.)
  6. Start to bring your attention to the breathing sensations throughout your body (expanding and contracting of the ribs and abdomen, rise and fall of the shoulders, etc.)
  7. Narrow your focus to the breathing sensations at the tip of your nose, wherever the sensations are the clearest.
  8. Keep your attention here, while maintaining a gentle awareness of your body and your surroundings.

Know this: your attention will wander, over and over and over again. Be diligent and patient. Gently redirect your attention to the object of focus each time you notice your mind wandering and soon your mind will learn to do this on its own. To keep the mind engaged with the breathing sensations at the nose, try to silently note both of the following:

  • the length of the pauses between the inhale and the exhale, and
  • the exact moment each breath begins and ends (both the inhales and the exhales).
