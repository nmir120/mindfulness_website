Mindful Living Practices

Apart from meditation, there are numerous ways to develop mindfulness. This page will discuss three of my favourite mindful living practices: doing "nothing", gratitude, and thoughtful media consumption.

Doing "Nothing"

This isn’t exactly as simple as it sounds, though it is by no means difficult. If you think meditation is too challenging, try starting off by doing nothing. This is a practice that involves finding a space to sit down and letting your mind do whatever it pleases. The only rule is that if you notice that you are consciously trying to do something (e.g., pushing away a thought or feeling, forcing yourself to think happy thoughts, etc.), let go of this effort. I would recommend starting off with 10 minutes per session and working your way up to 20 minutes.

This practice is so great because it lets you...

  • naturally gain a better understanding of the nature of thoughts (their flow and impermanence),
  • settle down your mind so you can think more clearly, and
  • understand that you do not need to act on certain thoughts or feelings; they pass on their own.

Gratitude

Multiple studies have shown that people who practise some form of gratitude lead happier lives. In our “default” day-to-day thinking, we tend to always be on the pursuit to achieve. “I need to get this thing, then I will finally be happy!” How many times have you convinced yourself of this? This type of thinking is simply misleading. You get the thing that you wanted, there’s a temporary sense of achievement and satisfaction, and then the next moment your eyes shift to another prize to pursue.

By practising gratitude, you learn to appreciate what you already have in the present moment. This type of mindfulness shifts your thinking to come from a place of abundance rather than a place of lack. You may find yourself no longer needing things you previously thought were essential, and better yet, you will find yourself experiencing joy from things you previously paid no mind to. Here are two ways to practise gratitude:

  • Sticky notes: You can write simple messages such as “What are you grateful for, right now?” or “Where can you find joy in the present moment?” on sticky notes and place them throughout your house. Every time you pass by one of these notes, make the conscious effort to answer the question.
  • Daily or weekly brief reflection: At the beginning or end of every day (or every week), take a few minutes to write out what you are grateful for. This could be absolutely anything; there are no rules. Just write down whatever comes to mind and encourage the feeling of gratitude to grow.

Mindful Consumption of Media

Dangers of Social Media

Do you compare yourself to others more often than you’d like? Are you unsatisfied with your achievements? Do you feel the constant need to prove yourself to others? Do you think the world is doomed? If you are answering yes to any of these questions, social media may be a culprit to your problems. Some platforms such as Instagram and Facebook are undoubtedly great at keeping you connected with friends and family and can provide entertainment during times of boredom, though, it is easy to dismiss the negative effects that these platforms might have on your mind. Naturally, as you scroll through these apps, you’ll find yourself looking at other people’s online personas, which you may have convinced yourself are accurate depictions of real people. You might wonder why others’ lives are so great and why yours is so boring. This type of thinking is the antagonist to the gratitude we discussed earlier.

You should also be aware of the negativity bias on social media platforms. That is, negative events overshadow positive events, even if there are just as many impactful positive events as there are negative ones. Which of the following news is more likely to go viral? An athlete donating to help build a school for deaf children, or a musician being murdered in a random street shooting. With this biased nature of media, we are quicker to form an overall negative image of the world, which is likely to have numerous negative effects on our well-being and mindfulness, such as distrusting strangers, stereotyping, and lacking faith in humanity.

How to Be More Mindful With Media & How Media Can Help You Be More Mindful

Firstly, it is wise to be mindful of how much time you spend on social media. These platforms tend to be designed in a way to keep you hooked so you spend as much time as possible browsing them. Now that you are aware of the negativity bias, next time you see good news online, take a moment to let it sink in and fully appreciate it. Secondly, look for and follow media that promotes mindfulness and related teachings. For example, one of my favourite podcasts is called “Secular Buddhism” by Noah Rasheta. Each episode discusses ideas from Buddhism and educates the listener on how they can apply mindful living principles to their own lives (the podcast does not require or even suggest that you become a Buddhist). Instead of hopping onto Instagram first thing in the morning, I made it a habit to plug in my earphones and listen to this podcast instead and it has done wonders for my mental well-being and my ability to act mindfully throughout the day.
